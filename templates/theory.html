001 // THE INSPECTION GAME

This game is based on a mathematical model called the Inspection Game. It models the interaction between a Smuggler (who wants to break the rules for profit) and an Inspector (who wants to catch them but pays a cost to inspect).

Here is the Payoff Matrix:

Inspector: INSPECT Inspector: SLEEP
Smuggler: SMUGGLE Smuggler: -5 (Caught)
Inspector: +5 (Success)		 Smuggler: +10 (Profit)
Inspector: -5 (Failure)
Smuggler: LAY LOW Smuggler: -1 (Lost Time)
Inspector: -2 (Wasted Cost)		 Smuggler: 0 (Neutral)
Inspector: 0 (Neutral)

002 // MIXED STRATEGY NASH EQUILIBRIUM

In this game, there is no single "best move". If you always Smuggle, the Inspector will always Inspect, and you will lose. If you never Smuggle, the Inspector will stop inspecting to save money, leaving free profit on the table.

The solution is a Mixed Strategy: You must randomize your actions. Mathematically, you should Smuggle exactly enough to make the Inspector indifferent between Inspecting and sleeping.

>> TACTICAL INSIGHT If the Inspector is catching you too often, you are likely too predictable. Try using a random number generator (or flipping a coin) to decide your move!

003 // SIGNALING & CHEAP TALK

Can you trust a Smuggler? Signaling Theory explores how valid information is transferred.

  • TRUCE (Cheap Talk): Saying "I won't smuggle" is cost-free. Since it costs nothing, a Smuggler can easily lie. Therefore, it is a weak signal.
  • BRIBE (Costly Signal): Buying a bribe costs points (-3). Because it is expensive, it carries weight. It buys you immunity because it proves you are willing to pay to avoid inspection.
